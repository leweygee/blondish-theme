# Ibiza Nightlife 2026: The Complete Season Guide

**By Léa Dubois** | Updated March 2026

*I have been living on this island for over a decade, covering its nightlife as a music journalist and correspondent. I have been inside every major club, danced until sunrise on forgotten beaches, and watched entire musical movements start in rooms that held fewer than two hundred people. This is not a guide written by someone who visited Ibiza once on holiday. This is what I actually know.*

---

## What Makes Ibiza Nightlife Unlike Anywhere Else

There is no other place on earth where nightlife operates the way it does in Ibiza.

Other cities have clubs. They open at midnight, close at four, and everyone goes home. In Ibiza, the entire culture of the island revolves around the night. The schedule of your day — when you eat, when you sleep, when you swim, when you see your friends — is shaped by which club you are going to and which DJ is playing. The night is not an afterthought to the day. The night is the reason the day exists.

What makes Ibiza nightlife fundamentally different from Berlin, London, Amsterdam, or any other dance music city comes down to three things.

**First, the open-air element.** Most of the world's best clubs are underground, literally. Basements. Warehouses. Converted industrial spaces with no windows. Ibiza inverts that. At Amnesia, the roof opens. At DC-10, the terrace sits next to an airport runway. At Ushuaia, the entire club is outdoors in daylight. You are dancing under the sky. The Mediterranean air is warm on your skin. The sun is setting or rising or both. The sensory experience is completely different from anything you can get in a city.

**Second, the concentration of talent.** For five months of the year, the world's most important DJs are all on one island that is 45 kilometres long. In a single week, you might hear a four-hour set from a DJ who plays only two or three times a year, followed the next night by someone who is redefining an entire genre, followed the next afternoon by an artist who has been shaping this music for three decades. Nowhere else compresses this much talent into this small a space.

**Third, the audience.** The crowd in Ibiza is different. People fly in from sixty different countries. They have planned this trip for months. They know who is playing. They know the music. The energy on a dancefloor in Ibiza at three in the morning is unlike anything else because the people in that room chose to be there with an intensity you do not find in most club settings.

The Ibiza season 2026 carries all of that forward — and brings some significant changes. This guide covers everything you need to know.

## The Ibiza 2026 Season Overview: What's New This Year

The 2026 Ibiza season marks a notable shift in several directions. Here is what is changing and what is staying the same.

### The Big Headlines

**Ushuaia and Hi are continuing to dominate the daytime-to-nighttime pipeline.** The Palladium Group's dual-venue strategy — Ushuaia for the daytime open-air spectacle, Hi for the late-night deep sessions — remains the most commercially successful model on the island. Expect expanded programming on both sides, with several new weekly residencies announced for 2026.

**DC-10 remains untouchable for the purists.** Circoloco's Monday party continues to be the single most important weekly event in electronic music. If you are serious about the underground, this is still the heartbeat.

**Amnesia is leaning harder into melodic and organic house.** The venue that has historically alternated between trance eras, techno eras, and commercial eras is entering 2026 with a programming strategy that reflects the current dominance of [melodic](/journal/melodic-house-music-guide/) and Afro-influenced house music.

**The San Antonio renaissance continues.** After years of being dismissed as the "budget" side of the island, San Antonio and its surrounding areas have developed a genuinely interesting underground scene. Eden, the Savannah sunset strip, and several smaller venues are pulling crowds who want something different from the Playa d'en Bossa corridor.

**Sustainability is no longer optional.** Several venues have committed to measurable environmental targets for 2026, including reduced single-use plastics, renewable energy sourcing, and water reclamation. This is a subject close to my heart — BLOND:ISH co-founded [Bye Bye Plastic](/projects/bye-bye-plastic/) specifically to push this conversation within nightlife, and it is encouraging to see the industry moving.

### What Is New for Ibiza 2026

- Several venue renovations completed over the winter, particularly at Amnesia and Pacha, with improved sound systems and expanded outdoor areas
- New licensing regulations allow extended hours for certain outdoor events, meaning some beach club programming can now run until midnight rather than the previous 11pm cutoff
- An expanded water taxi network connecting Ibiza Town, Playa d'en Bossa, and San Antonio makes getting between venues easier and cheaper than ever
- The electronic music festival calendar has expanded, with new multi-day events in May and October extending the traditional season window

## Monthly Guide: Ibiza Season 2026 — May Through October

One of the biggest mistakes first-time visitors make is treating the Ibiza season as one undifferentiated block. Each month has its own character, its own crowd, and its own energy. Choosing when to go is almost as important as choosing where to go.

### May: The Awakening

**Best for:** People who want the island before it gets crowded. DJs doing warm-up sets. Lower prices.

May is when the island wakes up. Opening parties start rolling out in the second and third weeks. Not everything is open yet — some residencies do not begin until June — but the energy of a season beginning is electric. The weather is warm but not oppressive. The beaches are not yet packed. Restaurants still have tables.

The opening parties themselves are events. Ushuaia's season opener is always a statement. Hi's first night sets the tone for the summer. DC-10's Circoloco opening is a pilgrimage.

**Key events in May 2026:**
- Ushuaia season opening (mid-May)
- Hi Ibiza season opening (mid-May)
- DC-10 Circoloco season launch (late May)
- Various beach club soft openings along Playa d'en Bossa

**Practical notes:** Flights and accommodation are 30-50% cheaper than peak season. Many villa rentals have not yet hit minimum-stay requirements. The downside: some nights will feel quiet, and the water is still cool for swimming.

### June: Full Power

**Best for:** The sweet spot. Everything is open, the weather is perfect, and the peak-season crowds have not fully arrived.

June is my favourite month on the island. Every residency is now running. The programming is locked in. The DJs are fresh and excited — nobody is tired yet. The weather is reliably hot but not the 38-degree furnace of August. The crowds are knowledgeable and enthusiastic but the clubs are not at crushing capacity every single night.

If you can only come to Ibiza once in 2026, come in June.

**Key events in June 2026:**
- All major residencies in full swing
- IMS (International Music Summit) — the industry conference that brings the entire electronic music world to one place
- Sonar Festival satellite events
- First wave of [Abracadabra events](/projects/abracadabra/)

**Practical notes:** Book accommodation at least 6-8 weeks in advance. Flights from European hubs are plentiful and competitively priced. Restaurant reservations at popular spots (Amante, La Paloma, Es Torrent) should be made 1-2 weeks ahead.

### July: Peak Energy

**Best for:** People who want maximum intensity. The biggest lineups. The biggest crowds.

July is when Ibiza reaches full operational capacity. Every venue is running its flagship nights. Special guest appearances spike. The island's population effectively doubles. The energy is relentless — there is something happening every hour of every day.

The downside of July is also its upside: it is a lot. The clubs are packed. The roads are congested. Getting into certain venues without advance tickets is not guaranteed. If you thrive on intensity, July is extraordinary. If you prefer space and spontaneity, look to June or September instead.

**Key events in July 2026:**
- Peak-season special guest lineups across all venues
- Amnesia's flagship weekly events at full capacity
- Pacha's iconic Saturday nights
- Ibiza Jazz Festival (early July)

**Practical notes:** Everything is at peak pricing. Book accommodation and flights 2-3 months in advance. Pre-purchase tickets for any night you care about — walk-ups are risky. Bring water everywhere. Sunscreen is not optional.

### August: The Crescendo

**Best for:** Experiencing the absolute peak of the global club scene. People-watching. Special one-off events.

August is the month when the world comes to Ibiza. The demographics shift — more holidaymakers, more groups, more spectacle. This is when the special events happen: the one-night-only b2b sets, the surprise guest appearances, the parties that become the stories people tell for years.

It is also the most expensive month by far, the most crowded, and the hottest. Temperatures regularly exceed 35 degrees. The afternoon hours between 2pm and 6pm can feel brutal. Smart scheduling means planning your days around heat avoidance: beach in the morning, shade and sleep in the afternoon, clubs at night.

**Key events in August 2026:**
- One-off special events and surprise lineups
- Ibiza Gay Pride (mid-August)
- Peak [Abracadabra programming](/projects/abracadabra/)
- Highest-profile international DJ appearances

**Practical notes:** This is the most expensive time for everything — flights, accommodation, club entry, even restaurant prices. Expect to spend 40-60% more than June. Traffic is significant, especially on the Ibiza Town to Playa d'en Bossa corridor. Use water taxis when possible.

### September: The Connoisseur's Month

**Best for:** Experienced Ibiza visitors. Closing parties. The best weather-to-crowd ratio.

September is the month that Ibiza insiders choose. The families and casual tourists have gone home. The schools have started. The people who remain are the ones who came specifically for the music. The dancefloors tighten. The energy becomes more focused. The DJs play longer, deeper sets because the crowd demands it.

The closing parties begin in mid-September and run through early October. These are major events — artists play extended sets, lineups are stacked, and the emotional weight of ending a season gives the nights an intensity that opening parties rarely match.

**Key events in September 2026:**
- Closing party season begins (mid-September)
- Extended DJ sets across all venues
- Final [Abracadabra gatherings](/projects/abracadabra/) of the season
- Ushuaia closing party (late September)

**Practical notes:** Prices drop back toward June levels. The weather is still warm — often 28-32 degrees — but the evenings cool off beautifully. This is arguably the best month for combining daytime beach life with nighttime club life. Book closing party tickets early; they sell out.

### October: The Afterglow

**Best for:** The closing ceremonies. Off-season early adopters. People who want a quieter island.

By early October, most major venues have held their closing parties. The island begins its seasonal exhale. Some clubs and restaurants close entirely. Others shift to weekend-only programming. The expat community reasserts itself. The pace slows.

But October in Ibiza is not dead. The final closing parties — particularly DC-10's Circoloco closer, traditionally one of the last major events — are legendary. And the island itself, freed from the crush of peak season, reveals a quieter beauty: empty beaches, unhurried meals, drives through the interior without traffic.

**Key events in October 2026:**
- Final closing parties (early October)
- DC-10 Circoloco season closer
- Amnesia closing weekend
- Transition to off-season programming

**Practical notes:** Flights are cheap. Accommodation is cheap. Many businesses operate on reduced hours or close entirely after mid-October. The weather can be unpredictable — warm days interspersed with occasional rain. Bring a jacket for the first time since April.

## The Super Clubs: Ranked and Reviewed

This is the section everyone wants. Here is an honest assessment of every major Ibiza club, based on more than a decade of attending and covering them. For deeper detail on each venue — including genre breakdowns, insider tips, and beach clubs — see our [complete guide to the best clubs in Ibiza](/journal/best-clubs-ibiza/).

### [Hï Ibiza](https://www.hiibiza.com/)

**Location:** Playa d'en Bossa
**Capacity:** ~5,000
**Best for:** State-of-the-art production, world-class sound, marathon sets
**Typical hours:** Midnight – 6am

Hï replaced the legendary Space in 2017 and has established itself as arguably the best club in the world for pure sound quality and production. The Theatre room is an immersive experience — the sound system is extraordinary, the lighting design is theatrical, and the layout creates an intimacy that belies the venue's size.

The Club room runs a different vibe, typically more underground and harder. Knowing which room to be in at which time is half the skill of a Hï night.

**Insider tip:** The terrace between rooms is where the night's social life happens. Do not spend your entire night locked in one room. Move. Float. The building rewards exploration.

### Ushuaia Ibiza Beach Hotel

**Location:** Playa d'en Bossa
**Capacity:** ~5,000
**Best for:** Daytime clubbing, outdoor spectacle, production shows
**Typical hours:** 4pm – midnight (some events start earlier)

Ushuaia changed the entire concept of Ibiza nightlife when it proved that people would dance just as hard at four in the afternoon as at four in the morning. The open-air pool stage, the hotel rooms overlooking the dancefloor, the sunset behind the DJ booth — it is a production that nobody has successfully replicated anywhere else in the world.

The music programming leans toward the bigger names: Fisher, David Guetta, Calvin Harris, Martin Garrix. But the undercard is often where the real discovery happens. Pay attention to the opening DJs.

**Insider tip:** The VIP sections offer shade, which matters more than status when it is 35 degrees. Also: wear shoes you do not care about. The pool area gets wet.

### DC-10

**Location:** Near the airport, outside Ibiza Town
**Capacity:** ~1,500
**Best for:** Pure underground music. The credibility benchmark.
**Typical hours:** Varies by event — Circoloco typically 4pm – midnight (Monday) or midnight – 6am

DC-10 is the antidote to everything else on this list. No LED screens. No pyrotechnics. No VIP tables. Just a concrete room with an extraordinary sound system and a crowd that cares about one thing: the music.

Monday's Circoloco party has been running for over two decades and remains the single most important weekly event in electronic music. The lineups are always impeccable. The vibe is loose, unpretentious, and genuinely joyful.

**Insider tip:** Tuesday morning after Circoloco Monday is when the real Ibiza reveals itself. The after-party culture around DC-10 is its own ecosystem. Bring comfortable shoes and no plans for the next 24 hours.

### Amnesia

**Location:** Between Ibiza Town and San Antonio
**Capacity:** ~5,000
**Best for:** Epic room experiences, the famous open-roof terrace, historical weight
**Typical hours:** Midnight – 6am

Amnesia is the club with the most historical gravity on the island. The terrace — where the roof opens to reveal the sky — is one of the most iconic spaces in all of dance music. When the sun comes up and you are standing in that room with two thousand people and the light is pouring in, you understand why people keep coming back to this island.

The main room is a different beast: dark, loud, intense. It is best suited for harder, driving music. The programming alternates between rooms in ways that create a natural ebb and flow to the night.

**Insider tip:** Arrive before 2am if you want to experience the terrace before it gets too crowded to move. The terrace at sunrise is the defining Ibiza moment.

### [Pacha](https://www.pacha.com/)

**Location:** Ibiza Town (marina area)
**Capacity:** ~3,000
**Best for:** Glamour, iconic history, and a different crowd energy
**Typical hours:** Midnight – 6am

Pacha is the oldest continuously operating club on the island, and it wears its history with pride. The cherry logo is an Ibiza symbol. The venue itself — with its multiple rooms, balconies, and intimate corners — has a character that no purpose-built superclub can replicate.

The music programming at Pacha has always been eclectic, ranging from deep house to disco to tech house depending on the night. The Solomun +1 residency (if it returns for 2026) remains one of the island's most sought-after tickets.

**Insider tip:** Walk to Pacha from Ibiza Town after dinner. It is a ten-minute walk from the port area. Arriving on foot, with the harbour lights behind you, is part of the experience.

### UNVRS (Formerly Privilege)

The venue formerly known as Privilege — and before that, Ku — once held the record as the largest nightclub in the world. Now rebranded as UNVRS, the massive space in San Rafael has undergone significant reinvention. It hosted Freddie Mercury's legendary birthday party and the original Manumission parties. Check the current season programming for what is happening in this iconic space.

### Eden

**Location:** San Antonio
**Capacity:** ~1,500
**Best for:** Underground programming, emerging talent, San Antonio's scene
**Typical hours:** Midnight – 6am

Eden has quietly become one of the most interesting venues on the island. The San Antonio location means lower prices and a different crowd than the Playa d'en Bossa corridor. The sound system was upgraded significantly in recent years, and the programming favours underground house and techno.

**Insider tip:** Combine an Eden night with sunset at Cafe Mambo or Savannah on the San Antonio strip. Watch the sunset, eat, then walk to the club. It is the best-value Ibiza night out.

## Beach Club Culture: The Daytime Scene

The Ibiza club guide would be incomplete without the daytime dimension. Beach clubs are not a sideshow — they are a core part of how the island's nightlife ecosystem works.

### The Major Beach Clubs

**Beachouse (Playa d'en Bossa)**
The long-standing afternoon institution on Bossa beach. The music programming is consistently good — deep house, organic sounds, and selective programming that respects the setting. Good food, reasonable prices by Ibiza standards.

**Nassau Beach Club (Playa d'en Bossa)**
More upscale than Beachouse, with a restaurant-forward approach. The music is background to the dining experience until mid-afternoon, when the energy lifts.

**Experimental Beach (Cap des Falco)**
On the southern tip of the island near the salt flats. This is the most visually striking beach club setting in Ibiza — wild, windswept, cinematic. The music programming tends toward Balearic and organic house. It feels like a different island entirely.

**Cala Gracioneta (near San Antonio)**
Tiny, hidden, and perfect. Not a "beach club" in the commercial sense — more a restaurant on a cove with occasional DJ sets. This is where you go when you want the real Ibiza.

### How Beach Clubs Fit the Nightlife Schedule

The typical Ibiza day-to-night flow works like this:

1. **Morning (10am – 1pm):** Beach, swim, recover
2. **Afternoon (1pm – 5pm):** Beach club — food, drinks, afternoon DJ sets
3. **Sunset (5pm – 9pm):** Either remain at beach club for sunset programming or move to a sunset bar (Cafe del Mar, Savannah, Kumharas)
4. **Dinner (9pm – midnight):** Late dinner (this is Spain — nobody eats before 9pm)
5. **Club (midnight – 6am):** Superclub night
6. **After-hours (6am – noon):** Optional and advanced-level

This rhythm is not a suggestion. It is how the island actually operates. Fighting it — trying to eat dinner at 7pm, arriving at the club at 10pm — will leave you out of sync with everything around you.

## The Underground Scene and After-Hours

For every person who comes to Ibiza for the super clubs, there is another who comes for what happens in the spaces between them. The underground scene is the island's soul, and it is thriving in 2026.

### Where to Find the Underground

**Pikes Hotel** — The legendary hotel in San Antonio hills that has been hosting parties since the 1980s. The pool parties at Pikes are intimate, weird, and wonderful. Freddie Mercury celebrated his 41st birthday here. The spirit of that era persists.

**Las Dalias (Saturday Market and Night Market)** — Primarily known as a hippy market, Las Dalias also hosts music events that lean toward world music, downtempo, and organic house. The setting is magical: outdoor, surrounded by trees, lanterns hanging overhead.

**Underground parties in the north** — The north of the island (around San Juan, Benirrás, and the rural interior) has a rotating cast of unlicensed and semi-licensed events that happen in fields, fincas, and forest clearings. These are found through word of mouth, not Google. Talk to people. Follow local Instagram accounts. Show up to the places where the DJs go on their nights off.

**Cova Santa** — A venue built into a natural cave system between Ibiza Town and San Jose. The acoustics are extraordinary. The programming favours deeper, more experimental music. This is one of the most unique physical spaces I have ever experienced.

### The After-Hours Culture

Ibiza's after-hours scene is legendary and operates in a grey area between official and unofficial. After the clubs close at 6am, the energy does not simply stop. It migrates.

Common after-hours destinations include certain beach areas (particularly in the north), private villa parties, and the handful of bars and clubs that have extended licensing. The details change every season — that is part of the point. After-hours Ibiza is not something you can pre-plan. It is something you find by being in the right place at the right time with the right people.

**One genuine piece of advice about after-hours:** know your limits. Ibiza is a marathon, not a sprint. The people who burn out by Wednesday are the ones who tried to do everything every night. The people who are still dancing on Sunday are the ones who chose their nights carefully and slept when they needed to.

## The Practical Survival Guide

Over a decade of living here has taught me that the difference between a good Ibiza trip and a great one is almost entirely in the logistics. The music will take care of itself. The logistics are what you need to plan.

### Getting Around Ibiza

**Taxis:** Ibiza taxis are regulated and metered. They are reliable but expensive — a taxi from Ibiza Town to Playa d'en Bossa is around 15-20 euros. From Ibiza Town to San Antonio, 25-35 euros. Late-night surge pricing does not technically exist (meters are fixed), but finding a taxi at 4am outside a major club can involve a 30-60 minute wait.

**The Disco Bus:** The official late-night bus service runs between all major venues from midnight until 6am during peak season. Routes connect Ibiza Town, Playa d'en Bossa, San Antonio, and the major clubs. It costs a few euros and is genuinely the smartest way to get around at night. Download the schedule in advance.

**Water Taxis:** The expanded 2026 water taxi network is a game-changer. Routes between Ibiza Town, Figueretas, Playa d'en Bossa, and (new for 2026) the San Antonio bay run from afternoon until late evening. It is faster than road transport, more scenic, and competitively priced.

**Scooter Rental:** Popular with returning visitors but comes with serious risks. Ibiza's roads are narrow, poorly lit at night, and full of people who have been drinking. I strongly recommend against riding scooters after dark. During the day, they are the fastest way to explore the island.

**Car Rental:** Useful if you are staying outside the main nightlife corridors or want to explore the north of the island. Parking near clubs is difficult to impossible during peak hours. Designate a driver or use the car for daytime only.

### How Much Does Ibiza Nightlife Cost in 2026?

Budget transparency matters. Here is what you should expect to spend:

| Item | Price Range (2026) |
|------|-------------------|
| Club entry (advance ticket) | 30 – 80 EUR |
| Club entry (door price) | 40 – 100 EUR |
| Drink in a superclub | 15 – 20 EUR (beer/basic spirit) |
| Water in a club | 8 – 12 EUR |
| Cocktail in a beach club | 15 – 25 EUR |
| Lunch at a beach club | 40 – 80 EUR per person |
| Dinner at a mid-range restaurant | 50 – 90 EUR per person |
| Taxi (average ride) | 15 – 35 EUR |
| Disco Bus | 3 – 4 EUR per ride |
| Hostel/budget accommodation | 40 – 80 EUR per night |
| Mid-range hotel | 150 – 300 EUR per night |
| Luxury hotel/villa (per person, shared) | 200 – 500+ EUR per night |

**Realistic daily budget breakdown:**
- **Budget traveller:** 100 – 150 EUR/day (hostel, pre-drinks, one club entry, disco bus)
- **Mid-range:** 250 – 400 EUR/day (hotel, beach club lunch, dinner, club entry, taxis)
- **No-limit:** 500 EUR+/day (luxury accommodation, VIP, multiple venues)

**Money-saving tips:**
- Buy club tickets in advance — always cheaper than the door
- Pre-drink at your accommodation or at a bar in Ibiza Town (drinks are 5-8 EUR vs 15-20 EUR in clubs)
- Eat lunch as your main meal — beach club lunch is expensive but dinner is more expensive
- Use the Disco Bus for all late-night transport
- Visit in June or September instead of July/August

### Dress Codes

Ibiza club dress codes are more relaxed than in most European cities, but they exist.

- **Superclubs (Hï, Ushuaia, Pacha, Amnesia):** Smart casual. No flip-flops, no football shirts, no stag-do costumes. Trainers are fine if they are clean. Shorts are fine at daytime events but less appropriate at midnight venues.
- **DC-10, Eden, underground events:** Wear whatever you want. Nobody cares.
- **Beach clubs:** Swimwear is fine during the day. Cover up slightly for dinner service.

### Safety

Ibiza is generally very safe. Violent crime is rare. The main risks are:

- **Dehydration and heat exhaustion.** This is the number one health risk for Ibiza visitors. Drink water constantly. Not just at the club — all day. The combination of heat, alcohol, dancing, and possibly not enough sleep creates a dehydration spiral that sends people to hospital every single week of every single season. Carry water. Drink it.
- **Hearing protection.** Please wear earplugs. Club-quality sound systems are running at 100+ decibels for six hours. One night will not permanently damage your hearing. A week of nights might. Inexpensive concert earplugs (Loop, EarPeace, or similar) reduce volume without destroying sound quality. Every DJ I know wears them, and so do I when I am covering a night.
- **Sun exposure.** You are on a Mediterranean island at the latitude of North Africa. SPF 50 is not overcautious, it is baseline. Reapply every two hours at beach clubs.
- **Substances.** I am not going to pretend this does not exist. If you choose to partake, test your substances (testing kits are cheap and available), do not mix, do not take anything from strangers, stay hydrated, stay with friends, and know where the nearest medical facility is.
- **Theft.** Pickpocketing happens in crowded clubs and on beaches. Do not bring more than you need. Use a money belt or front pocket. Leave passports and valuables in your accommodation safe.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Ibiza Nightlife

### When does the Ibiza 2026 season start and end?

The Ibiza season 2026 officially runs from mid-May through early October. Opening parties for major venues typically happen in the second and third weeks of May. Closing parties run through September and into the first week of October. Some venues and beach clubs operate on extended shoulder seasons from April through November, but the full nightlife programming is concentrated in the May-October window.

### How much does a week in Ibiza cost?

A one-week Ibiza trip for a mid-range traveller — including flights from a European hub, hotel accommodation, daily meals, two or three club nights, and daytime activities — typically costs 1,500 to 2,500 EUR per person. Budget travellers sharing hostel rooms and being selective about club nights can do it for 800 to 1,200 EUR. There is effectively no upper limit.

### What is the best month to visit Ibiza for nightlife?

June and September are the best months. June has full programming, perfect weather, and manageable crowds. September has the closing parties, the most dedicated music crowds, and lower prices than peak summer. July and August offer maximum intensity but come with maximum crowds and maximum prices.

### Do I need to book Ibiza club tickets in advance?

Yes. For popular nights — particularly weekend events at Hï, Ushuaia, and Amnesia, plus any night at DC-10 — you should book tickets one to four weeks in advance. Walk-up entry is possible on quieter midweek nights, but for anything you specifically want to attend, pre-purchase is strongly recommended. Tickets are available through venue websites and authorised resellers.

### Is Ibiza nightlife safe for solo travellers?

Yes. Ibiza is one of the safer nightlife destinations in Europe. The clubs are well-managed, the local infrastructure is geared toward tourism, and the community is welcoming. Solo travellers should take standard precautions: stay aware of your surroundings, do not accept drinks from strangers, keep your phone and wallet secure, and share your plans with someone. Many people travel to Ibiza solo specifically because the communal dancefloor culture makes it easy to connect with others.

### What time do Ibiza clubs open and close?

Daytime clubs (Ushuaia, beach clubs) typically open between 2pm and 4pm and close at midnight. Night clubs (Hï, Amnesia, Pacha, DC-10, Eden) open between midnight and 1am and close at 6am. Some events have special hours — check individual event listings. The important thing to understand is that arriving at a nightclub before 1:30am means arriving to an empty room. The real energy does not start until 2am or later.

### What should I wear to Ibiza clubs?

Smart casual for the super clubs: clean trainers or shoes, shorts or trousers, and a decent top. No flip-flops, no football shirts, and nothing you would wear to the gym. For daytime events at Ushuaia, swimwear with a cover-up works. For underground venues like DC-10, anything goes. The general rule: look like you thought about it for thirty seconds, and you will be fine everywhere.
